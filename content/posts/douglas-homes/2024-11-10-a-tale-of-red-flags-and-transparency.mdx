I want to share the story of my first attempt at buying a new home in Calgary. The builder was Douglas Homes, and the experience taught me hard lessons about transparency and due diligence.

The property was a spec home at **204 Langwell Common SE**, located in **Lanark Landing** — a community developed by **Melcor** in Airdrie, Alberta. The lot was Phase 4, Block 13, Lot 1. My salesperson was **Trish Braun**.

On November 12, 2024, I met with Trish, confirmed my initial **$5,000 bank draft** deposit was received, and signed the purchase agreement. The remaining balance of the 10% deposit was due ten business days from the contract date, after conditions were waived. At the time, I felt optimistic. That didn't last.

**Red Flag #1: No sample contract, no warranty.**

Before signing, I had asked to review a sample purchase contract. Douglas Homes refused. Then, after signing, Trish stated that **Schedule C (Home Warranty) was not currently attached** and that I wasn't entitled to view it until closing. Being asked to commit to a legally binding agreement without seeing the home warranty was like boarding a plane without knowing the safety protocols.

**Red Flag #2: HOA misrepresentations.**

During our November 12 meeting, Trish assured me there were no restrictions on security cameras — any issues would fall under city bylaws. She confirmed no restrictions on fire pits unless there was a fire ban. She said the fence height restriction was the primary HOA concern, and there was nothing else I needed to be aware of. She also confirmed there were no restrictive covenants after Melcor left, and maintenance of parks, streets, and boulevards would fall to the City of Airdrie. These assurances would prove to be incomplete at best.

**Red Flag #3: My name was wrong on legal documents.**

Trish repeatedly misspelled my name on the legal documents and refused to correct it. When I pressed the issue, I was told that changing my name on the documents now would be "extra work" and that they would handle it with a future change request — presumably at additional cost. On a $600,000+ purchase. My name.

**Red Flag #4: DocuSign from a personal email.**

The emails and documents came from a personal email address, not a Douglas Homes corporate account. The contract was e-signed by "DW" through a numbered Alberta corporation's DocuSign — not Douglas Homes' own system. This raised immediate questions about the legitimacy and security of the transaction.

**Red Flag #5: The bathtub specification.**

My Schedule A specified a **[Fiberglass Hytec AC759-O](https://www.hytec.ca/product-detail/AC759?skuid=AC759-0)** bathtub. After reviewing the specs, its near-full-height dimensions were a critical requirement for me. This detail would become important as the deal unravelled.

**Red Flag #6: The 8-inch plot plan discrepancy.**

The most critical issue was that the spec home was built **eight inches narrower** than permitted by the architectural guidelines of Lanark Landing. The discrepancy fell under **Section 3.2.2** of the guidelines, which specified minimum lot widths. I asked Douglas Homes to contact **E2 and Associates**, the architectural committee for Lanark Landing, to obtain a formal waiver as defined by **Section 10** of the guidelines. They refused.

**Upgrades we discussed.**

During the November 12 meeting, we also discussed upgrades: tile in the walk-in closet (around **$720**), a gas line to the back of the house for approximately **$300** placed as close to the end of the deck as allowed, and Cat 6 runs in the flex room, above the fireplace, in the primary bedroom, and two in the basement bedrooms. The appliance package included an electric stove — a gas line was not available except for the furnace. The basement ceiling height was eight feet.

**The collapse.**

Over eight days — from signing on November 12 to the final call on November 18 — every conversation raised new concerns rather than resolving old ones. By November 20, the deal was dead. My deposit was refunded.

Looking back, I wish I had applied more rigorous due diligence from the start. I wish I had demanded the home warranty and a sample contract before ever putting money down. My earnest appeal to anyone reading this: seek transparency and demand answers. Do not sweep red flags under the rug.

— Colton English
