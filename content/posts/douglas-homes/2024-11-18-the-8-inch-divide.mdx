I've always believed that buying a home should be one of life's most exciting milestones. When I signed with Douglas Homes on November 12, 2024 for a spec home at **204 Langwell Common SE** in **Lanark Landing** (a Melcor community in Airdrie), I was filled with that optimism. But what unfolded over the next eight days was a cautionary tale about how even the smallest details, when overlooked or mishandled, can unravel everything.

I put down a **$5,000 bank draft** as my initial deposit. My contact was **Trish Braun**. Her confidence was comforting at first, but that comfort was short-lived.

**The contract and the warranty wall.**

The first crack appeared when I requested a sample purchase contract before signing. I was met with resistance — a reluctance to share details upfront. After signing, Trish told me that Schedule C (Home Warranty) was not attached and that I wasn't entitled to view it until closing. For someone who values clear communication, this was deeply unsettling.

**The 8-inch discrepancy.**

The issue that shattered the deal was an eight-inch discrepancy between the architectural guidelines and the as-built dimensions of the home. The spec home was built eight inches narrower than permitted under **Section 3.2.2** of the Lanark Landing architectural guidelines, which specified minimum building widths for the lot size.

Eight inches sounds trivial. In homebuilding, where precision defines property boundaries and future modifications, it was a significant deviation.

I pushed hard for a formal waiver. On November 18, I made four calls:

1. **To Trish Braun** — requesting that Douglas Homes pursue a waiver. She refused and said the approval stamp on the plans meant the builder was responsible for ensuring compliance, but that head office wouldn't provide further documentation.[^1]

2. **To Erin at Melcor** — to understand the waiver process. I pointed out that the approval stamp stated the builder was responsible for ensuring the building aligned with all covenants, and that what Douglas Homes was asking for wasn't a waiver as defined by **Section 10** of the architectural guidelines.[^2]

3. **To E2 and Associates** — the architectural committee for Lanark Landing. I explained that the spec home was built eight inches narrower than permitted by the guidelines, and that Douglas Homes didn't want to contact E2 to resolve the issue. The ideal solution would be for Douglas Homes to obtain a waiver for the discrepancy as outlined in Section 10.[^3]

4. **Final call with Trish** — In this call, Trish reiterated that head office wouldn't provide further documentation and that my options were to proceed with the purchase or accept a refund of my deposit.[^4]

**Other troubling signs.**

Along the way, I discovered misrepresentations about HOA rules and restrictive covenants that were never fully disclosed. The DocuSign came from a personal email address through a numbered Alberta corporation — not Douglas Homes' official system. My legal name was misspelled on the contract, and correcting it was treated as "extra work."

**The collapse.**

After the final conversation on November 18, where Douglas Homes reiterated their refusal to provide any waiver or resolution, the deal collapsed on November 20. What had started as a hopeful step toward homeownership ended abruptly.

This experience taught me a hard lesson: buying a home demands transparency, respect, and clear communication. It's not about rushing into the flashiest or most convenient option — it's about ensuring your concerns are heard and addressed with honesty.

To anyone considering Douglas Homes or any builder: insist on clarity. Ask for sample contracts before you commit. Verify every detail. And demand formal resolutions to any discrepancies. Your future home deserves that level of care.

— Colton English
