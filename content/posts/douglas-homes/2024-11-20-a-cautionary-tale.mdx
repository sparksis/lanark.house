Today, November 20, 2024, my first attempt at buying a new home officially ended. The builder was Douglas Homes. The property was **204 Langwell Common SE** in **Lanark Landing**, a Melcor community in Airdrie. The salesperson was **Trish Braun**. I want to tell you what happened, because I hope it helps you avoid the same experience.

**The beginning.**

I signed the purchase agreement on November 12 and put down a **$5,000 bank draft**. The home was a spec build — Phase 4, Block 13, Lot 1 of Lanark Landing. My Schedule A specified a **[Fiberglass Hytec AC759-O](https://www.hytec.ca/product-detail/AC759?skuid=AC759-0)** bathtub, whose near-full-height dimensions were a critical requirement. We discussed upgrades including tile in the walk-in closet (around **$720**), a gas line to the back of the house (approximately **$300**), Cat 6 ethernet runs on the main floor and in the basement, and an eight-foot basement ceiling height.

**The red flags I ignored.**

Right from the start, Trish refused to provide a sample contract before signing. After signing, she told me Schedule C (Home Warranty) was not attached and I wasn't entitled to view it until closing. The contract documents came from a personal email address, e-signed by "DW" through a numbered corporation's DocuSign — not Douglas Homes' own system.

**Misrepresentations and the name game.**

After signing, I discovered that the HOA rules and restrictive covenants were not what Trish had represented during our meeting. On top of that, my legal name was wrong on the contract. When I asked for a correction, I was told it would be "extra work" and deferred to a future change request.

**The 8-inch discrepancy.**

The deal-breaking issue was an eight-inch discrepancy between the as-built home and the minimum building width under **Section 3.2.2** of the Lanark Landing architectural guidelines. I asked Douglas Homes to obtain a formal waiver from **E2 and Associates** (the architectural committee) under **Section 10** of the guidelines. They refused.

**The final call.**

On November 18, I had a final phone call with Trish. I opened the call by stating: **"This is a recorded phone call."**[^1] Trish's response to the recording was straightforward — she didn't object. But her position was clear:

> "In speaking with the management group at head office, there's nothing more they can provide as it relates to this… it's up to your comfort level, if you're comfortable going ahead. Otherwise, they can certainly refund your deposit."

And then:

> "There's not gonna be any more discussions about my DocuSign and the e-mail that it came from, or any more discussions on the plotting of the house on the lot. It is what it is."

**"It is what it is."** On a $600,000+ home purchase. On questions about the legitimacy of the signing process and the compliance of the plot plan. That was the answer.

**Why I'm sharing this.**

When you're trading 30 years of your life for a home, you expect a certain level of respect and professionalism. You expect to feel like a valued partner in the process, not a nuisance. My experience with Douglas Homes was the opposite. The deposit was refunded, and I moved on. But the lesson stayed: demand transparency, document everything, and never let a builder tell you that your concerns about a legal agreement are simply "what it is."

Stay informed, stay cautious, and good luck on your home buying journey.

— Colton English
