This is the final post in my home-buying journey series. I'm writing this from 728 Langley Terrace SE in Airdrie — the Sterling Homes "Luna" that I took possession of on June 2, 2025 — and I want to look back at the trail I traveled to get here.

My path to homeownership ran through three builders over about seven months:

1. **Douglas Homes** (November 2024) — I signed a contract for a spec home at 204 Langwell Common SE in Lanark Landing, a Melcor community in Airdrie. I put down a $5,000 bank draft. Over eight days, I discovered an 8-inch plot plan discrepancy, a refusal to provide the home warranty before closing, and a salesperson who sent contracts from a personal email through a numbered corporation's DocuSign. The deal collapsed on November 20 when Douglas Homes refused to seek a formal waiver under Section 10 of the architectural guidelines and offered to refund my deposit instead.

2. **Jayman Built** (March–May 2025) — I received a purchase agreement on March 4, 2025 for an Avid 20 with revised ensuite at 71 Legacy Reach Court SE in Calgary's Legacy community. I paid $7,820 for the revised ensuite and $12,600 for the Fit and Finish Extra package. Their brochure promised "80" height, chrome frame, 10 mil glass shower door" — but the contract was vague, and when I asked for clarification, the specs kept shrinking: first to 70"/5mil, then 75"/6mil. After 16 days of silence from the sales manager and a formal dispute letter to the VP, Jayman terminated the agreement in early May.

3. **Sterling Homes** (May–June 2025) — My first contact was Jason Tang, and the difference was immediate. No sales pressure, straight answers. The purchase agreement was comprehensive, every schedule was present, and the home warranty was included as a matter of course. CIBC approved my mortgage on May 21. The home inspection on May 28 with Marty Keough from Nook & Cranny came back clean. I took possession on June 2.

Here's what I learned.

**Lesson One: Demand the contract before you sign anything.**

Douglas Homes refused to provide a sample contract or the home warranty (Schedule C) before closing. That's not standard practice — it's a red flag. If a builder won't let you review the full agreement and all schedules before you put money down, walk away.

**Lesson Two: The brochure is not the contract.**

Jayman's glossy Fit and Finish brochure described an 80" shower with 10mil glass. The contract referenced "other shower stalls applicable to plan" without specifying dimensions or materials. When there's a gap between what's advertised and what's written, the written contract governs — and builders know this. Get every specification that matters to you written into the agreement explicitly.

**Lesson Three: Record everything.**

I recorded phone calls (with notice), saved every email, and documented every conversation. When Douglas Homes told me to "just trust them," the recordings told a different story. When Jayman's specs changed between April 10 and April 11, the emails were the evidence. Documentation isn't paranoia — it's due diligence.

**Lesson Four: A quick-possession home can be the right move.**

After two failed attempts with pre-construction homes, Sterling's quick-possession Luna model meant I could see exactly what I was buying. I was settled into my home before Jayman had even broken ground on the Legacy lot, and long before the Douglas Homes property would have been completed. The home was more affordable and included the features that mattered most to me, including full-height tile in the bathroom — the very detail that was the centre of my dispute with Jayman.

**Lesson Five: The sale is just the beginning.**

Post-possession brought its own challenges: a site supervisor (Mike) who dismissed concerns and provided dangerously wrong information about foam off-gassing, a warranty app (Virtuo) with a critical Android bug, and an eavestrough issue that took weeks to get addressed. The warranty phase tested my patience more than the buying phase. Be prepared to advocate for yourself after the keys are in your hand.

This journey has been about more than buying a house. It's been about understanding the value of a 30-year commitment — the exchange of time for security, and the importance of finding a builder who respects that. My hope is that by sharing this story, I can help you find a builder who will honour your commitment and help you build a home that is truly worth the exchange.

— Colton English
