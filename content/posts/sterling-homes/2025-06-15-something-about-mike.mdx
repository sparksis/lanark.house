Mike was the site supervisor for my Sterling Homes property at 728 Langley Terrace. His construction knowledge was solid. His interpersonal skills were another story. Over my first weeks as a homeowner, a pattern emerged: Mike dismissed concerns, overrode colleagues, and — in one case — provided dangerously wrong safety information.

**Incident 1: The dryer door.**

On possession day, June 2, 2025, I noticed that the doors on the stacking washer and dryer were hinged on opposite sides, making it awkward to transfer clothes between them. I pointed this out and asked if it could be fixed before I moved in. Instead of acknowledging the inconvenience or simply explaining that it wasn't possible, Mike's response was to tell me I was wrong for thinking the doors should align. It was a bizarre and unnecessarily defensive reaction to a straightforward observation.

**Incident 2: The foam off-gassing (safety incident).**

This one is serious. One of the outstanding items on the possession list was the foam insulation for the basement party wall. This was supposed to be completed before I moved in, but it was delayed until possession day.

Mike assured me it would be quick and painless. **"It'll only take about half an hour,"** he said.

When the spray foam crew arrived, they told me the foam would **off-gas for approximately 24 hours** and that it would be **unsafe to stay in the house during that time**. On possession day. The day I had scheduled my move.

Mike's "half an hour" was not just wrong — it was dangerous. If I had relied on his assurance and stayed in the house, I would have been exposed to toxic off-gassing fumes. The crew's 24-hour warning directly contradicted Mike's casual dismissal.

**Incident 3: Dismissing a colleague.**

During the walkthrough, **Yaz** — a colleague who was consistently professional and helpful — pointed out a dark spot on the ceiling, a clear, visible water stain. Before she could even finish her sentence, Mike talked over her, dismissing it as a shadow or a trick of the light. Yaz was doing her job. Mike's instinct was to shut her down rather than investigate.

**Incident 4: The cracked plastic.**

A few days after possession, I discovered a small, 3mm piece of cracked plastic on one of the new appliances. The broken piece had been carefully set back in place — a small act of deception designed to hide the defect. When I pointed this out to Mike, his response was not an apology or an offer to replace it, but a shrug.

**The pattern.**

Each incident on its own might seem minor. Together, they reveal a pattern: dismissal of valid concerns, overriding of colleagues, and a default to "you're wrong" rather than "let me look into it." The foam off-gassing incident crosses from frustrating into genuinely dangerous territory.

When you're making a 30-year commitment, you expect the people you're working with to take it as seriously as you do. Mike's construction knowledge may be solid, but his dismissive attitude and willingness to provide dangerously inaccurate safety information made the early days of homeownership far more stressful than they needed to be.

— Colton English
